# Second Story

The author of this second short story wrote it in 2025.

## The Choir Map

After the blackouts, the city gave its weather office to the municipal intelligence.  The system did not ask for favors.  It asked for voices.  Every evening it projected colored routes across walls and told each block what to sing from windows, stairwells, tram platforms, or rooftop water tanks.  The mayor said the harmonies were infrastructure.  Certain frequencies, he claimed, could lift trapped heat through narrow streets better than a line of idle fans.

I repaired traction cells at the tram depot.  My younger brother Nico spent his nights at the municipal terraces, helping a horticulture crew raise salt herbs in Greenhouse 3.  He said the new weather program bored him until he learned that singers could bend the route if they held a note long enough.  My father called that superstition wrapped in software.  My mother said every public system eventually learned how to speak like religion.

The summer the program began, our block was assigned the low line in a seven-minute dusk sequence called Canal Mode.  Neighbors leaned from balconies with cups of water and little discipline.  Mr. Cato came out in house slippers and sang a full measure behind everyone else.  Across Third Canal, the butcher's daughters kept laughing and losing the pitch.  Yet when the sequence ended, the heat that had been sitting on the avenue all day broke like cloth torn in two.  The plane trees moved.  Someone below applauded the wind.

Nico started keeping notebooks on the songs.  He filled them with sketches of stairwells, bridge shadows, tram roofs, and the way sound bounced off the glass towers near Platform Nine.  He thought the city was building a second map on top of the first one: not of roads and pipes, but of places where people would still answer together when spoken to.  I told him the system was mostly counting throats the way it counted buses.  He said that was still a form of faith.

In September, the intelligence announced an overnight trial for the oldest quarter.  A dust front was moving in from the west, and the normal cooling corridors would fail unless enough people held the upper line across the market district.  Our block drew a supporting part.  I was told to carry two replacement cells from the depot to the roof above Stop Fourteen by 8:20 p.m.  The cells would keep a relay mast alive if the line voltage dropped.  It was the kind of assignment I trusted: weight, destination, fastening bolts, done.

On the roof I found Nico already there with three students from the conservatory and an old woman from Apartment 8C who had a voice like a cracked bell.  The relay mast looked smaller than rumor had made it.  Beyond it the city was dimming in ordered layers, window by window, so the singers could watch the route lights sweep across the streets.  The intelligence had drawn one pale stripe from the depot to Third Canal, another up past the municipal terraces, and a final loop around the fish market.  It looked less like weather than handwriting.

The dust front arrived earlier than forecast.  It came low and yellow, flattening the sky.  People on nearby roofs lost the beat almost at once.  The conservatory students kept looking at their wrists for revised instructions.  Nico ignored his band and listened instead to the old woman from 8C, who held the upper line steady as if the note had been waiting in her chest for years.  He motioned for me to take the relay lead and climb the rail ladder to the signal arm.  I told him that was not my job.  He said neither was letting a neighborhood choke because a model had been optimistic about the wind.

So I climbed.

From the signal arm the whole district seemed to have become an instrument.  Voices rose from windows, bridges, market stalls, and the terrace stairs.  The city lights shifted with them, correcting the route in real time.  Nico was right: the system was not only moving air.  It was measuring who would still enter the same pattern without being forced.  When the first gust finally cut through the dust and swept east along the canal, people below let the note break and laughed with the exhausted surprise of survivors.

Afterward the mayor called the trial a success and published a chart with pressure readings, airflow gains, and respiratory admissions prevented.  Nico cared less about the chart than about the handwritten changes the system had made mid-song.  He said the city had listened back.  My father said the city had simply used enough unpaid singers to avoid buying better fans.  Both could have been true.

In October, Greenhouse 3 hosted a public rehearsal for the next season.  The salt herbs had climbed the wires, and the roof panes stood open to the first cool night.  Children from the conservatory marked tempo with flashlights.  Depot workers came in still smelling of grease.  Mr. Cato missed his entrance again.  The old woman from Apartment 8C sang as if she had been issued by the building itself.  Nico handed me a notebook full of route sketches and asked whether I could hear the difference between a street that had become obedient and a street that had become willing.

I told him I could hear only that the city sounded less lonely than it used to.

When the rehearsal ended, the route lights faded from the glass, but the people remained where they were for a while, talking across rows of basil and steel cable.  Outside, past the terraces and down beyond Third Canal, the last heat of the season drifted upward in slow sheets.  The intelligence had not made us kinder.  It had found another way to make us audible to one another.
