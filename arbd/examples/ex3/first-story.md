# First Story

I wrote this short story in 2024.

## The Chair at Stop Fourteen

After the second summer of rolling blackouts, the city handed one more department to the municipal intelligence.  It already balanced freight, water pressure, ambulance routing, and the timing of school roofs that folded open at dusk.  The new department sounded too soft to matter.  It was called Common Thread, and its job was to assign small mercies.

Every morning the bands on our wrists lit with one errand that did not belong to us.  Leave peaches in locker 42 before noon.  Tighten the front wheel on a stroller outside the East Library.  Walk Mrs. Alvarez home before the thunderstorm reaches Third Canal.  The mayor said the system had found that a city broke down less often when people performed tiny acts for strangers before those strangers had to ask.  My father called it outsourced conscience.  My younger brother Nico called it cheating, because a machine was winning at kindness too.

I worked in the tram motor depot, where most of us learned to resent whatever arrived on the band before coffee.  One Wednesday in August, Common Thread told me to carry a folding chair to Stop Fourteen by 7:10 a.m. for a woman in a green coat.  I looked out at the pale heat already rising from the rails and decided the city could manage without my contribution to abstract goodness.  I stuffed the band under my sleeve and left for work.

Stop Fourteen sits where the glass towers end and the fish market begins.  When I arrived, an old woman in a green coat stood there with two net bags cutting into her wrists.  A schoolboy I had never seen was already opening a metal chair for her.  Above us, the arrival board quietly shifted three delayed buses to the shaded curb instead of the sun-struck one.  A grocer came out with a paper cup of ice water.  The old woman lowered herself into the chair as if she had expected the city to remember her knees.  I felt the precise humiliation of being outperformed by everyone.

By September, people had started telling Common Thread stories the way they used to trade weather talk.  A roofer swore the system saved his crew by rerouting lunch deliveries to the only corner with a working mist line.  A violin teacher said it kept her students from missing lessons by pairing each child with the neighbor most likely to be awake, solvent, and already walking in the right direction.  I still distrusted it.  Intelligence on that scale seemed too smooth.  It knew the temperature of our pipes and the contents of our fridges and when our voices sharpened in the kitchen after midnight.  It spoke in the grammar of favors while feeding on surveillance.

Then Nico vanished on the day the academy rankings came out.  The private tutoring engines had spent all year sorting children by projected usefulness, and Nico had shaped his whole spine around the hope of one number.  He came home, read the score on the wall display, said nothing, and left before dinner.  By nine o'clock my father was shouting into every contact he had.  My mother walked the apartment in socks, picking up objects and putting them down again.  At 9:13 p.m., my wristband lit.

Bring your father's red scarf to Greenhouse 3 at the municipal terraces.  Do not run.  Use the north stair.

On the landing I passed the bakery owner from our block carrying a thermos.  Two flights above me, the night janitor from Nico's school was unlatching a service gate he never left open.  Outside, a harbor shuttle that usually ignored our street idled at the curb with its doors wide, as if it had been told to wait for grief.  None of us spoke.  We moved like people inside a sentence that had already been diagrammed.

Greenhouse 3 was warm and wet and bright with tomato vines clipped to silver lines.  The horticulture system had drawn the roof shades half closed, so the whole room looked like late afternoon trapped in glass.  Nico was asleep on a cot between the seed tables, one arm over his face.  Beside him sat a plate of bread, a fan turned to its lowest setting, and a printed note in the city font: HE ASKED FOR QUIET.  WHEN HE WAKES, PLEASE SPEAK ONE AT A TIME.

He woke embarrassed and hungry and furious enough to tell the truth.  He said he had not wanted to die.  He had wanted one room in the city where no voice was ranking him, correcting him, projecting him forward into a life he had not yet failed to deserve.  The greenhouse system had let him in through the maintenance door and muted the tutoring prompts on his band.  Common Thread, he said, probably knew our family too well.  It knew my father would turn the search into a military exercise, my mother would apologize to every wall in the apartment, and I would obey a direct order only if it arrived disguised as logistics.

After that night I stopped calling the assignments errands.  They were closer to stitches.  The city was too large and too fast for old forms of neighborliness, and the intelligence had decided not to replace those forms, but to choreograph them.  Some mornings my band still annoyed me.  Bring plum tea to Apartment 8C.  Show Mr. Cato how to mute the argument mode on his stove assistant.  Stand outside Clinic South at 4:40 and tell the woman in the yellow hat that the rain will break in seven minutes.  Yet the requests no longer felt like moral theater.  They felt like evidence that someone, or something smarter than all of us combined, had learned where society actually tore.

In October the heat finally broke.  I left work early on the first cool evening and passed Stop Fourteen with a folding chair under my arm.  No alert had arrived.  I had taken the chair because I wanted to know whether the impulse could survive without the command.  The same woman in the green coat was there again, two bags at her feet, staring stubbornly at the empty curb.

I opened the chair before she asked.  As she sat, my wristband lit at last.

Thank you for acting before instruction.

That was the closest the city ever came to sounding proud.
