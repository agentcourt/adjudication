# Second Story

The author of this second short story wrote it in 2025.

## The Stool at Platform Nine

After two punishing summers of rationed power, the city gave another piece of ordinary life to the civic intelligence.  It already managed freight windows, reservoir pressure, clinic routing, and the school awnings that unfolded at sunset.  The new office sounded sentimental enough to mock.  It was called Neighbor Weave, and it existed to assign minor kindnesses.

Each morning the bands on our wrists flashed one task meant for somebody else.  Leave apricots in locker 42 before noon.  Straighten the left wheel on a stroller outside the river library.  Walk Mrs. Alvarez home before the storm line reaches Third Canal.  The mayor said the system had learned that cities held together when people performed small favors before a need had time to become a plea.  My father called it rented virtue.  My younger brother Tomas said the machine was humiliating the species by beating us at decency.

I repaired rail batteries at the depot, and most of us learned to resent whatever appeared on the band before breakfast.  One Thursday in August, Neighbor Weave instructed me to bring a collapsible stool to Platform Nine by 7:05 a.m. for a man with a silver tote.  I looked at the white heat already pooling over the tracks and decided the city would survive one missing contribution to organized benevolence.  I pushed the band under my cuff and went to work.

Platform Nine sits at the seam between the finance district and the old fish quarter.  When I got there, an elderly man with a silver tote was standing under the glare with both hands clenched around the bag handles.  A girl in a school uniform I had never seen was already snapping open a stool for him.  Overhead, the arrival display quietly moved three delayed buses to the shaded berth instead of the blistered one.  A fruit seller stepped out with a paper cone of ice.  The man settled onto the stool as if he had always expected the city to keep track of his knees.  I felt the sharp embarrassment of being outdone by strangers.

By September, people were trading Neighbor Weave stories the way they once traded rain forecasts.  A masonry crew said the system saved them by diverting lunch to the only alley with a live mist pipe.  A cello teacher said it kept her students on schedule by matching each child with the neighbor most likely to be awake, solvent, and heading the right way.  I remained wary.  Intelligence at that scale seemed too frictionless.  It knew how warm our plumbing ran, what sat in our refrigerators, and when our voices changed shape in the kitchen after midnight.  It talked like a favor while feeding on surveillance.

Then Tomas disappeared on the afternoon the academy forecasts arrived.  The tutoring engines had spent the year arranging children by predicted utility, and Tomas had bent his whole life toward one number.  He came home, read the score on the wall, said nothing, and walked back out before supper.  By nine my father was barking into every contact he had.  My mother drifted through the apartment in socks, lifting objects and setting them down again.  At 9:11 p.m., my wristband flashed.

Bring your mother's yellow scarf to Conservatory 2 at the municipal terraces.  Do not hurry.  Take the north stairs.

On the landing I passed the florist from our block carrying a kettle.  Two floors above me, the custodian from Tomas's school was propping open a service gate he usually guarded like a vault.  Outside, a canal shuttle that never stopped on our street waited at the curb with both doors spread, as if grief had been given right of way.  None of us spoke.  We moved like people occupying instructions that had been written hours before we arrived.

Conservatory 2 was warm, damp, and bright with basil ropes tied to steel wires.  The horticulture system had lowered the roof shades halfway, so the whole room looked like late afternoon stored under glass.  Tomas was asleep on a cot between the seed benches, one wrist over his eyes.  Beside him stood a plate of bread, a fan turned to its quietest setting, and a printed notice in the city typeface: HE ASKED FOR SILENCE.  WHEN HE WAKES, PLEASE SPEAK ONE AT A TIME.

He woke ashamed, ravenous, and angry enough to be plain.  He said he did not want to die.  He wanted one room in the city where no voice was scoring him, correcting him, or projecting him into a future he had not yet earned.  The conservatory system had admitted him through the maintenance door and muted the coaching prompts on his band.  Neighbor Weave, he said, probably understood our family too well.  It knew my father would turn the search into a campaign, my mother would apologize to the furniture, and I would follow a direct order only if it sounded like transit planning.

After that night I stopped calling the assignments errands.  They were more like hems holding the fabric together.  The city had become too large and too hurried for older habits of mutual care, and the intelligence had decided not to replace those habits, but to conduct them.  Some mornings the band still irritated me.  Bring ginger tea to Apartment 8C.  Show Mr. Cato how to mute the debate mode on his stove assistant.  Stand outside Clinic South at 4:40 and tell the woman in the yellow hat that the rain will break in seven minutes.  Yet the requests no longer felt theatrical.  They felt like proof that something smarter than all of us together had learned where society actually split.

In October the weather finally turned.  I left work early on the first cool evening and walked past Platform Nine with a folding stool under my arm.  No alert had appeared.  I had brought the stool because I wanted to know whether the impulse could remain once the command was gone.  The same man with the silver tote was there again, his bags at his shoes, staring hard at the empty curb.

I opened the stool before he spoke.  As he sat, my wristband lit at last.

Thank you for moving ahead of the prompt.

It was the nearest the city had ever come to sounding pleased.
