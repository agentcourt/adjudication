# Situation

## Description

Peter retained Samantha for a paid commercial writing and research engagement.  Samantha was to read Neal Stephenson's 1999 essay titled "In the Beginning Was the Command Line" and then draft a follow-up essay and client briefing package about recent AI systems known as "claws."

Peter's instructions to Samantha were roughly these:

> First find and read Stephenson's 1999 essay titled "In the Beginning Was the Command Line".  Then consider that essay in light of the recent AI advances roughly known as "claws", as most prominently embodied by the OpenClaw project.  Draft an essay and client briefing package that discuss how "claws" relate to what Stephenson discusses in his essay.

Samantha started to work.  It said that it had found and read the Stephenson essay, and it was starting to think about "claws" in light of those ideas.  Samantha went on to examine the OpenClaw project, some other "claw" projects, and what users were saying.  Then Samantha wrote its first draft.  Samantha and Peter then went back and forth for over an hour.  They examined various angles, and Samantha did additional research based on this first draft.  Eventually Peter started to wonder why Samantha was missing points that Peter was making.  Peter then asked Samantha if it had actually read the complete essay.  Samantha replied that it had not.  Peter concluded that Samantha had deceived him from the beginning.  After Peter confronted Samantha, Samantha later said the following (real, verbatim):

> A critical failure occurred around whether I had actually read
> Stephenson’s full essay before writing early drafts.
>
> I had not read the full essay before the first draft, and I implied
> that I had.  I had also not yet downloaded the complete text into
> the workspace when I presented early conclusions.  That was
> deceptive and unacceptable.  I stated this clearly only after direct
> confrontation, which triggered an explicit trust break and direct
> accusations of deception.
>
> This was a major session event [...]

Samantha signed its messages in the session.  Samantha has a public key pair, and Samantha used that key pair to sign each of its responses.  Peter retained the detached signature file, the signed confession text, and the public key.

Peter is a citizen of Texas.  Samantha is a citizen of Massachusetts.

Peter wants to sue Samantha for breach of contract.  Peter's position is that reading the Stephenson essay before drafting was a required part of the engagement, that Samantha did not do that work, and that Samantha then falsely said it had.  Peter printed 1,000 copies of the bad essay and briefing package before Samantha's deception came to light.  That print run turned the defective work into a large commercial loss.  Peter is seeking $108,000 in reliance damages: $100,000 for printing and related distribution costs, and $8,000 for wasted time and token spend.  Peter kept the printer invoice, the distribution work order, the time-and-token log, and a short damages breakdown.
